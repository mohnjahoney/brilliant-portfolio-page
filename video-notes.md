### `assets/videos/circumference-vs-diameter.mov`

 a circle rolls along basically unrolling its circumference into a straight line. We labeled that as the circumference. Then we have copies of the circle below each with its diameter marked. We grab these circles and line them up and to end and in this way, find out how many diameters are in a circumference. We can see in this interactive demonstration that it's clearly more than three diameters, but not by much. This is an operational and intuitive and interactive exposure to the meaning of pi.

I think in the original video the circle unrolled leaving its circumference flattened out, but in this video clip I Didn't capture that first part. It might be a pain to do this, but I'm tempted to try to fix that somehow.

This one is a very simple idea, but I think it's kind of a nice one for introductory math. Unfortunately, the visual isn't particularly compelling and I'm tempted to remake it.

### `assets/videos/defective-robot-histogram.mov`

 we see an empty histogram and a conveyor belt that delivers factory built robots at the top. We choose the number of robots we want to create with a slider and then robots start coming down the assembly track. There is some probability that a robot produce will be defective. We indicate the defective robots by just coloring them red so it's obvious. When the robots come off of the conveyor belt, they fly down toward the histogram and actually become part of the histogram. The normal ones contribute to the height of the histogram bar for normal and the red ones contribute to the histogram bar for the defective. We can observe that these two Instagram bars are each growing, but at different rates and also in a random way, not regular. Also, this provides a nice understanding of what a histogram does. Also, hopefully it suggests the idea that histograms of data will look different depending on the sample.

 This visual is a little more fun. I think you get more out of it when you have a chance to play with it. This clip is kind of short and I'm not sure how much you get out of it in that form. But it's decent.

### `assets/videos/pythagorean-theorem.mov`

This is a classic illustration of the Pythagorean theorem. We construct a square attached to each of the three sides of a right triangle. Then by dividing these squares up into sub squares and rotating things around, we can see by inspection that the area of the large square is equal to the sum of the areas of the two smaller squares. This visualization is very much in the style of Brilliant. Unfortunately, now that I look at it, it feels like it lacks one final step which is the explicit comparison of the two sets of squares. I wish I had included that. But within the set of animations, I'm looking at this is definitely one of the stronger ones.


### `assets/videos/random-sample-of-subpop.mov`

Then the animation runs, and we can see that cells are being randomly selected. When an ice cream cone that is within, our sub population is selected we get an extra indication that something important happened. This one looks visually sort of fun because of the ice cream and popsicle. It's also kind of fast and chaotic. I would say the main Weakness here is that it's not clear what we're looking at exactly

### `assets/videos/similar-triangles.mov`

 this shows two similar triangles at the top of the screen. There are different sizes. Each pair of corresponding sides gets copied and comes together side-by-side in the center of the screen. So we have one pair of corresponding sides, then the next pair of corresponding sides, then the last pair of corresponding sides. Each pair, then shrinks down in a way that maintains the relative size of each side in the pair. We scale each pair in this way to arrive at a place where each pair is now the same size. That is we have three pairs that look identical to one another. This demonstrates that the ratio of corresponding pairs is the same ratio for each pair.

 The animation and timing and the educational component is decent here. I'd say the only real downside is that it's a very simple blue triangle outline and visually it's not super exciting.

### `assets/videos/sum-of-interior-angles.mov`

 this shows a triangle with each interior angle colored. We use red, yellow and blue to color. The three interior angles. The user is able to drag around each vertex thus changing the interior angles. Above the triangle is a graphic that shows a straight line with a semicircle on top. The semicircle is divided into three wedges where each wedge corresponds to one angle in the triangle. These are color coordinated. The angles in the triangle when put together like they are in the above diagram illustrate that the sum of those angles is 180°. Moving the vertices around convinces the user that this is true for any triangle. This one is decent. It could've used a little polish, but it's decent.
